Reusable Instruments

The interview protocol and codebook, offered as reusable instruments — explicitly to enable replication in other regions and internationally.

The study deliberately focused on U.S. emergency managers operating under U.S. doctrine (NIMS, ICS, FEMA). International EM runs on different frameworks — for example, the UN cluster approach — and may surface different requirements. These instruments are shared so others can run the extension.

What’s here

  • Interview protocol — the full semi-structured script (background → decision scenarios → resource-constrained scenarios → protective-action and evacuation scenarios → improvement opportunities → recovery-center planning).
  • Pre-interview questionnaire — AI-familiarity items adapted from the Pew Research Center’s survey, plus optional demographics.
  • Codebook (v3.0) — five top-level themes: Decision-Making Scenarios, Technology Use Cases, EM Context, Current Tech Usage, and Future Tech Imaginings.

Design choices worth reusing

  • Funnel structure — open with their background and a real disaster they handled; introduce technology and AI only later. Grounding participants in concrete experience before speculation yields more valid responses.
  • Critical-incident / scenario-based elicitation — ask participants to walk through a specific decision (process, data consulted, stakeholders, outcome) before asking evaluative questions about where AI could help.
  • Tailoring by AI familiarity — for participants who use AI at work, probe specifics; for those who don’t, focus on general technology experience and hypotheticals.
  • Reflexive thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke) — open coding generated 847 initial codes; iterative constant-comparison rounds synthesized them into the codebook and seven use-case categories.

Adapting internationally

If you’re replicating outside the U.S.:

  1. Swap the doctrine-specific references (NIMS/ICS/FEMA, ICS form numbers, EMAP) for your jurisdiction’s equivalents.
  2. Re-examine the recovery-center section (Disaster Recovery Centers / MARCs are U.S. constructs).
  3. Keep the funnel and critical-incident techniques — they’re framework-agnostic.
  4. Report your sample’s AI familiarity with the same validated items to enable cross-study comparison.

If you run an extension, the authors would value hearing about it — open an issue at https://github.com/samsaranc/ai-for-emergency-management/issues.
